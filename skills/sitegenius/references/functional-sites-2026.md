# Functional Sites Research — Conversion Patterns (July 2026)

Companion to the Awwwards files. This one covers sites that *work* — SaaS marketing sites, local business, booking, pricing, e-commerce. Directly relevant to the $350 RNR product: these patterns close clients and generate their leads, which is what gets referrals.

**Rating key** — Steal /10 = conversion impact per hour of build effort for RNR-tier clients.

---

## 1. The 10-Second Homepage Test — Steal 10/10 · Every RNR site

Contractor/local research: a homepage must answer three questions within ~10 seconds or the visitor leaves — **Can you fix my problem? Can I trust you? How do I contact you?** Traffic quality won't save a page that fails this. Build order for every RNR hero: what-they-do headline → trust signal (reviews/certs/years) → contact CTA, all above the fold at 375px.

## 2. Only 4 Elements Move the Needle — Steal 10/10 · Every RNR site

Across 28,000+ A/B tests, most page variance comes from four elements, in order: **headline, hero image, primary CTA, form**. Tests on these hit significant wins ~24% of the time vs 13% overall. Sequencing rule: perfect these four before touching anything else on a page.

- Benefit-led headlines beat feature-led by **27%**; adding a concrete number adds another **15%**.
- CTA above the fold in the hero: **20–30% lift**. Repeat the SAME CTA (same destination) mid-page and at the bottom — consistency reduces cognitive load.
- CTA copy matters disproportionately: one word change ("Sign up for free" → "Trial for free") = **+104%** trial starts in one case study.

## 3. Three-Field Forms — Steal 10/10 · Every RNR site

HubSpot data: 3-field forms convert ~25%, ≤5 fields converts **120% better** than ≥10. Collect name + email (+ phone for trades) only; qualify after the lead exists. Our current name/email/message form is right — never let a client add "how did you hear about us" to the first touch. (Sell this stat on calls.)

## 4. Three-Tier Pricing with Anchor + Badge — Steal 9/10 · RNR's own site + client pricing pages

- Three tiers is the number — two gives no anchor, five causes paralysis. One team went 5→3 tiers + "Most Popular" badge: **1.2% → 3.1% conversion (+158%)** with no price change.
- High anchor works even if nobody buys it: adding a $500 Enterprise tier lifted the $99 tier **+28%** with zero feature changes.
- Stack: high anchor first (or right), "Most Popular" on the target tier, 9-ending prices, social proof near the CTA.
- RNR's own pricing already does this ($300 audit anchoring the $350 site). For client sites with packages (salons, gyms, catering): always 3 options, badge the middle.

## 5. Mobile Converts Half as Well — Design for It — Steal 9/10 · Every RNR site

Mobile is ~70% of traffic but converts 40–51% worse than desktop across every study (landing pages: desktop ~4.8–5% vs mobile ~2.5–2.9%). Restaurants with genuinely mobile-first sites see **40% higher conversion** than "responsive as an afterthought." This is the money argument for the existing 375px hard rule: big tap targets, click-to-call in the sticky header, no PDF menus, forms that don't zoom-jump.

## 6. Local Business Non-Negotiables Checklist — Steal 10/10 · LocalSite template

From restaurant/contractor 2026 research — bake into the LocalSite base template:
- **Click-to-call** phone number (`tel:` link) visible without scrolling
- **NAP** (name, address, phone) consistent everywhere + Google Maps embed
- **Hours** prominent; **HTML menu/services list** (never PDF)
- **Reviews/testimonials** on the homepage; certifications for trades
- **Quote/booking form above the fold** for contractors; before/after project gallery; **service-area map or list** ("do you cover my zip")
- **Structured data** (LocalBusiness schema) + Google Business Profile link
- Page set for trades: home / one page per major service / about / contact / gallery / service-area pages — each serves a different search intent. (Supports the $350 3-page core + per-page upsell.)
- Stat for sales calls: **68% of diners have skipped a restaurant because of its website.**

## 7. Booking Flow Rules — Steal 9/10 · Booking upsell ($125–$175)

Service-business data: median salon online-booking rate 26%, 90th percentile 61% — the gap is flow design, not demand.
- Hero explains **what booking delivers** (length, format, outcome), not just "Book now"
- Minimum fields to confirm; qualify after the booking exists
- 3 CTAs (hero / after social proof / bottom), all to the same booking destination
- Best-in-class service sites convert 7–12% vs 2–4% average — that's the pitch for the booking add-on ("high digital adoption ≈ $9,900/mo incremental per location" for the ambitious clients).

## 8. Product-First, Not Decoration-First — Steal 8/10 · Both tiers

2026 SaaS benchmark sites (Linear, Stripe, Vercel, Mercury, Ramp): the winners lead with **real product visuals** — actual screenshots/UI, not abstract illustration or stock. The local-business translation is already our rule: real food photography ("highest-ROI design element on any restaurant site"), real job-site photos, client work in a browser mock (our café hero). Never stock.

## 9. Pick One Aesthetic and Commit — Steal 8/10 · Both tiers

SaaS design 2026 split into two winning camps: **techno-futurist** (dark + neon + shaders + bento grids) and **editorial** (cream + serif + whitespace — exactly the RNR look). Both win; mixing them loses. Match camp to client: trades/restaurants/wellness → editorial; tech-ish local startups → techno-futurist. Dark bg + monospace reads as "technical credibility" to developer audiences only — don't use it on a salon.

## 10. Checkout / Transaction Friction — Steal 6/10 · Only if e-commerce work comes in

~70% of shoppers who reach checkout abandon; it's a UX problem, not pricing. One-page checkout beats multi-step on mobile by 20–30% (Shopify data). Guest checkout, autofill, wallet payments. Average e-com conversion 2.5–3%; food & bev converts highest (~6%). Park this until an e-commerce client shows up — don't build the capability speculatively.

## 11. Iterate, Don't Redesign — Steal 7/10 · RNR maintenance pitch

Linear refreshes incrementally several times a year; Stripe rewrites sections quarterly. "Continuous iteration beats big-bang redesigns for compounding conversion." This is the justification for the $19/mo–$149/yr maintenance plan: sell it as conversion iteration (swap headlines, test CTAs, refresh photos), not "we keep it online."

---

## The one-page summary

For RNR's market, conversion is won by: a hero that answers fix/trust/contact in 10 seconds → benefit headline with a number → one repeated CTA → 3-field form → click-to-call → real photos → mobile-first for real. Everything in the Awwwards files is in service of #2's "hero image" slot and the trust signal — spectacle earns attention; these patterns convert it.
